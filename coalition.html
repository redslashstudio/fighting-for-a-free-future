THE DISTRIBUTION PROBLEM

The Centre for Policy Studies, the Institute of Economic Affairs, the Adam Smith Institute, the TaxPayers' Alliance — between them, they've produced decades of rigorous research on monetary policy, taxation, civil liberties, trade, and the foundations of a free society.

The ideas are serious. The evidence is strong. And almost all of it is locked inside institutional packaging that the next generation will never open.

A brilliant paper published behind an institutional website, shared as a PDF link with a two-line summary, reaches the people who already agree. Nobody else.

The problem is structural, not intellectual. Think tank websites signal "institution" — white backgrounds, serif fonts, research grids, PDF downloads. That visual language triggers exactly the response you don't want from a 27-year-old professional scrolling on their phone: close the tab.

Content dies after publication. A paper on monetary reform published six months ago might be the most relevant thing to today's debate. But nobody's surfacing it. There's no living index, no way for a newcomer to navigate the body of work by issue. Every think tank is a silo, and the connections between their thinking are invisible to anyone who doesn't already know where to look.

THE NETWORK EFFECT

Right now, every think tank is a silo. CPS publishes on fiscal policy. IEA publishes on economic freedom. ASI publishes on deregulation. The TaxPayers' Alliance publishes on public spending. Each has its own website, its own audience, its own archive. The connections between their thinking are invisible to anyone who doesn't already know the landscape.

FFF's topic taxonomy — twelve issues across economics, trade, liberty, and politics — maps naturally to these specialisms. It becomes the connective tissue that makes work across organisations discoverable as a single, coherent body of thinking.

The more organisations participate, the stronger the system becomes. Every new think tank that feeds research into the knowledge engine makes every topic page richer. Every new paper processed through the social engine adds to the collective brand presence in feeds. Every new source indexed in the interactive archive makes the answers more comprehensive.

This isn't a platform that extracts value. It's shared infrastructure that creates it. Each think tank's work becomes more discoverable, more shareable, and more connected to the broader movement — without losing attribution or institutional identity.

No single think tank can build a shared distribution layer for the free-market movement. But together, through Fighting for a Free Future, the coalition's thinking becomes navigable, shareable, and queryable for the first time — reaching audiences that institutional packaging never could.

SHARED INFRASTRUCTURE, SHARED INVESTMENT

The distribution layer the free-market movement needs doesn't build itself. The commercial model is straightforward: think tanks invest in the infrastructure that makes their work reach further.

What think tanks get

  • Social distribution of research to audiences they can't reach alone
  • Modern, culturally relevant presentation of their ideas
  • Inclusion in the knowledge engine — research made navigable and discoverable by issue
  • Research indexed in the interactive archive — surfaced the moment it becomes relevant
  • Youth audience exposure and brand association with a high-quality media property

What FFF gets

  • A content pipeline grounded in rigorous research, not just editorial opinion
  • Coalition depth — serious institutions backing the platform with substance
  • Revenue to sustain and develop the shared infrastructure
  • Research evidence to underpin topic pages and social content
  • The credibility that comes from being the movement's chosen distribution layer

PARTNERSHIP MODELS

Three ways to structure the relationship — from individual participation through to collective investment:

Partnership Subscription

An annual fee for inclusion in the distribution engine, knowledge engine, and interactive archive. Tiered: from topic page links and archive indexing through to full social engine processing and featured placement.

Distribution Retainer

FFF processes a set number of think tank publications per month through the social engine. The think tank provides the research; FFF handles extraction, design, and distribution. Every piece credits the source.

Coalition Sponsorship

Think tanks collectively fund FFF's infrastructure as shared movement resources. Not purchasing a service — investing in the distribution layer the movement lacks. Same economics, different conversation.
