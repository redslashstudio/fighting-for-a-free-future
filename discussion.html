SYSTEMS

We built three systems. Then we put each one under the same question: does this actually serve the audience we're trying to reach? Three specialist perspectives. One honest evaluation.

THE THREE SYSTEMS

Each system serves a different function in the strategy of breaking intellectual groupthink through quality, visibility, and accessibility.

01
Earns attention

Social Distribution

Branded Instagram carousels extracted from Substack articles. The only system that goes to the audience — meeting them in their feed, on their phone, in the scroll.

02
Converts attention

Knowledge Engine

Topic pages that reorganise 211 Substack posts by issue, not by date. A newcomer can explore a position in three minutes and decide whether to go deeper.

03
Deepens engagement

Interactive Archive

AI-powered chat that answers questions from the full Substack archive. Turns the paywall from a wall into a window — explore 211 posts without committing to read them.

Lens One

THE AUDIENCE STRATEGIST

The visitor's journey matters more than the systems. Josh doesn't arrive thinking "I'd like to browse a knowledge engine." He arrives with a question, a mood, or a provocation ringing in his ears.

Social Distribution

The only system that goes to Josh

This is the most strategically important system by a significant margin. Josh will never type a URL into a browser. He'll see a carousel in his Instagram feed that a mate shared, or that the algorithm surfaced. Without the social engine, the other two systems are speaking to an empty room.

The risk is that carousels feel like political propaganda rather than something interesting enough to share without social cost. The permission framework applies here: the design quality has to make sharing feel culturally acceptable, not politically exposed. Josh shares things that make him look thoughtful, not things that make him look right-wing.

Knowledge Engine

Where Josh goes when the social content has worked

He's clicked through. He's on the site. He doesn't want to scroll through 211 chronological posts — he wants to explore by interest. The 60-second position format is exactly right: understand a stance quickly, see if it's credible, decide whether to go deeper.

But this only works if Josh has already been given a reason to arrive. On its own, the knowledge engine is a library with no front door. The topic pages currently read as comprehensive reference material. Josh doesn't want a reference — he wants a provocation that resolves into a position.

Interactive Archive

A novelty that could become a retention tool

Josh isn't going to arrive at the site and immediately open a chat with an AI. That's a behaviour reserved for people who already care. But for the Josh who has already read a topic page and wants to go deeper, this is a way of exploring 211 posts without the commitment of reading them.

The risk is that it occupies prominent real estate for a behaviour that most first-time visitors won't engage with. It's impressive as a demo, but Josh's 8-second question is "is this real?" — not "can I chat with their archive?" It may matter more for the disillusioned activists who already care, or the media insiders researching Baker's positions.

VERDICT

The audience hierarchy is clear: social engine first, knowledge engine second, interactive archive third. The social engine is non-negotiable — it's the only thing that reaches Josh. The knowledge engine is the destination the social engine needs to point to. The interactive archive is interesting but optional for Josh's journey.

Lens Two

THE BRAND STRATEGIST

The question isn't what these systems do. It's what they say about Fighting for a Free Future. Each system makes a brand argument — intentionally or not.

Social Distribution

FFF is a living, shareable media operation

This is the single most important brand signal the project can send. The difference between "Steve Baker has a Substack" and "Fighting for a Free Future is producing broadcast-quality content in my Instagram feed" is the difference between a political blog and a cultural presence.

The design system — Bebas Neue, navy/orange/cream, screen-print aesthetic — was built for this context. Legible at thumbnail size. Recognisable before you read a word. Every carousel that appears in a feed is a repetition of the visual identity that makes FFF feel real, serious, and distinctive.

What the carousels need to say: "Something interesting is happening here." Not "here's what we think about the chancellor." The best ones will make Josh screenshot and send to a mate — not because he agrees, but because it's interesting.

Knowledge Engine

FFF has a coherent worldview, not just opinions

This is what separates FFF from every other political commentator. Anyone can publish hot takes. Reorganising 211 posts into a structured worldview across 12 topics says: we've thought about this systematically. That's credibility for Josh, and credibility for the media and think tank world that FFF wants to influence.

The 60-second positions are the brand's intellectual calling card. If they're sharp, specific, and evidence-backed, they make the argument that FFF is a serious intellectual project. If they're vague or generic, they undermine everything the social engine built. The tone should be confident, not defensive — conclusions, not arguments.

Interactive Archive

FFF is technologically sophisticated

A secondary but real brand signal. Most political operations can barely keep a WordPress site running. An AI-powered archive search says "these people are building something different." For Josh — who works in fintech — this might register as a credibility signal.

But the brand risk is that it feels like a tech demo rather than a useful tool. If Josh asks a question and gets a mediocre answer, the brand signal inverts — from "sophisticated" to "gimmicky." The framing matters: "we've made it easy to explore four years of thinking," not "look, we have AI."

VERDICT

The social engine carries the brand into the world. The knowledge engine proves the brand has substance. The interactive archive adds sophistication that could impress or could feel like showing off. The first two are essential to the proposition. The third is a nice-to-have that needs to earn its place through quality of output.

There's a precedent for this. Bari Weiss left the New York Times because she believed the newsroom had become ideologically captured — the same structural groupthink this project diagnoses in British media. She didn't start a blog. She built The Free Press — a media brand with the design quality, editorial voice, and cultural confidence to make engagement feel like a statement of taste rather than a political act. The Free Press proved that you don't break through by arguing louder. You break through by building something so well-crafted that ignoring it becomes the harder position.

Lens Three

THE CREATIVE DIRECTOR

The broadcast-quality standard applies to all three systems. But the bigger question is coherence: do these three things feel like parts of one operation, or three separate experiments?

Social Distribution

The strongest creative expression of the brand

The carousel format is perfectly suited to the design system. Bebas Neue headlines at phone scale. Navy/orange/cream. Screen-print illustration style. These carousels should be the most visually distinctive political content on Instagram. Nothing else looks like this.

What makes it work: the constraint. 1080×1350. Six slides maximum. Cream for reading, dark for feeling. These constraints force editorial discipline and visual clarity. The format doesn't allow waffle — every slide has to earn its place.

The risk is repetition fatigue. If every carousel follows the same template rigidly, the feed becomes predictable. The format needs enough variation — cover image styles, slide types, colour alternation — to feel curated rather than generated.

Knowledge Engine

Solid foundation, needs editorial treatment

The structure works — 60-second position, evidence cards, related articles. But the design treatment should make these pages feel like something you'd want to read, not something you'd consult.

Each topic page should feel like opening a magazine feature on that subject. The 60-second position should land with the visual weight of a pull quote in a broadsheet. The evidence should feel curated, not listed. The transition to Substack should feel like going deeper, not clicking away to a different product.

The risk: if the topic pages are functional grids of content while the homepage is cinematic, the quality drops and Josh notices — even if he can't articulate why.

Interactive Archive

A different interaction paradigm

The rest of the site is composed — considered sections, visual rhythm, typographic hierarchy. The chat interface is, by nature, unpredictable. The answer quality depends on the retrieval system, not on design. That makes it the hardest system to bring up to broadcast quality.

AI chat interfaces are everywhere now. The novelty has worn off. If this feels like "we put ChatGPT on our website," it actively undermines the broadcast-quality standard. The answers need to feel authored, not generated. Source citations should feel like editorial footnotes, not search results. A mediocre AI experience is worse than no AI experience.

VERDICT

The social engine and knowledge engine are natural extensions of the design system — they feel like the same operation. The interactive archive is an outlier that needs significantly more design attention to feel like it belongs. Priority for creative investment: social engine first (it's the public face), knowledge engine second (it's the intellectual substance), interactive archive third (it's an experiment that needs to prove itself).

WHERE THE THREE LENSES AGREE

All three perspectives arrive at the same hierarchy. The disagreements are in emphasis, not direction.

HOW THE SYSTEMS COMBINE
