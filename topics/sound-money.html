Economics & Money

SOUND MONEY

Why the Bank of England needs fundamental reform

21 articles & episodes
THE 60-SECOND POSITION

Every crisis of the last two decades — the financial crash, the cost of living, the collapse of trust in politics — traces back to the same root cause: broken money.

Since 1971, when the last link to gold was severed, governments have funded promises they cannot afford by debasing the currency. Banks create money from nothing every time they make a loan. The Bank of England has admitted as much. Between 1997 and 2010, the UK money supply tripled. During the pandemic, the Bank simply printed money to cover government borrowing. And every time new money enters the system, it benefits those closest to it first — the City, the speculators, the asset-rich — while everyone else watches their savings erode and house prices climb beyond reach.

This is not a technical problem. It is a moral one. Sound money — money whose supply cannot be manipulated at will — is the foundation of a free society. It constrains the state, rewards honest work, and ensures that exchange is founded on value, not illusion.

That is why we advocate for gold and Bitcoin. Not because they are fashionable, but because they are principled. They protect the individual from arbitrary power. And if we are serious about a future that is free, prosperous and just, monetary reform is where it begins.

Key Evidence

  1. Between 1997 and 2010, the UK money supply tripled from approximately £700 billion to £2.2 trillion. During the pandemic, the Bank of England printed money to cover government borrowing directly.
    Sound money is the cornerstone of a free society — Steve Baker
  2. Whenever a bank makes a loan, it simultaneously creates a matching deposit in the borrower’s bank account, thereby creating new money.
    Bitcoin, gold and the Bank of England — Steve Baker (Bank of England Quarterly Bulletin, Q1 2014)
  3. UK prices remained broadly stable for over two centuries until 1971, when the last link to gold was severed. Since then, the value of money has collapsed.
    Sound money is the cornerstone of a free society — Steve Baker (ONS composite price index 1750–2003)
  4. New money flows disproportionately to large corporations and financial speculators first. By the time it reaches ordinary people, prices have already risen. This is the Cantillon Effect.
    Money needs reform — Steve Baker
