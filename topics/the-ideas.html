THE 60-SECOND POSITION

The fiscal crisis, the housing crisis, the collapse of trust in politics — these are not random failures. They are the predicted endpoint of a governing philosophy that has dominated Britain for a century. The thinkers who diagnosed this — Mises, Hayek, Bastiat, Popper — wrote decades ago. Their work is not academic history. It is a manual for understanding what is happening now and what must be done about it.

In 1922, Ludwig von Mises published a proof that socialist economic planning could not work — not because of corruption or bad intentions, but because without market prices there is no way to allocate resources rationally. A decade later he was warning that the “middle road” of interventionism was no stable compromise: each intervention distorts price signals, creating problems that demand further intervention, until the state manages everything and manages nothing well.

Hayek extended this into a warning about political consequences. The Road to Serfdom argued that centralised economic planning requires centralised political control — that the demand for order and efficiency leads, by degrees, to authoritarianism.

These were not abstract predictions. Britain today spends more than it has since the aftermath of the world wars. Taxes are at their practical ceiling. The currency has lost over 99% of its purchasing power since the gold link was severed. The managerial state that James Burnham described in 1941 — technocratic, unaccountable, confident in its own expertise — is the system we live under.

Bastiat saw the mechanism clearly in 1850. When the law is used not to protect life, liberty and property but to take from some and give to others, he called it “legal plunder” — and warned that once started, every group would fight to be the plunderer rather than the plundered.

Karl Popper offered the antidote — not a utopian blueprint but a method: the open society, where all claims to authority can be questioned and power is constrained by institutions that permit criticism without punishment.

Hayek’s essay The Intellectuals and Socialism may be the most important strategic text for anyone who believes in a free society. Socialism won not through superior economics but through the intellectual class — teachers, journalists, commentators — who filter every idea before it reaches the public. Classical liberals lost not because they were wrong, but because they stopped making the moral and cultural case.

Fighting for a Free Future exists because that case must be made again — and because this generation, armed with encryption, decentralised money, and open communication, has tools of resistance that previous generations lacked.
