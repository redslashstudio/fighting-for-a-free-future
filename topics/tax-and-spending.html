THE 60-SECOND POSITION

Britain’s fiscal crisis is a spending problem, not a revenue problem. Taxes are already at their highest sustained level since the Second World War — and the evidence says raising them further will shrink the economy faster than it fills the Treasury. The only honest path is to spend less.

The Taxable Ceiling

Research examining UK data from 1870 to 2019 found it has proved almost impossible to sustain a non-oil tax burden above 39% of factor-cost GDP. Even during the Second World War, when the economy was effectively centralised, the average was 37.5%. Britain was already at 37.6% before the pandemic — and Labour has pushed it higher still. Meanwhile, the non-dom tax abolition projected to raise £2.5 billion is estimated to cost over £10 billion a year in lost growth as capital and talent leave. At the ceiling, every extra pound of tax loses more than it raises.

Britain’s Fiscal Reckoning: The Inevitability of Spending Cuts — Harry Richer

The Austerity Myth

Public spending as a share of GDP has never fallen below 34% since before the Second World War. Even at the end of Thatcher’s premiership, spending had merely returned to comfortable post-war consensus levels. Cameron and Osborne’s so-called austerity brought it down to its long-run average — roughly the midpoint of Blair’s time in office. The story of “small state Britain” is a political narrative, not an economic fact. And if long-term productivity growth stays at 0.5%, the OBR projects debt reaching 647% of GDP by 2073.

Spending Review: The Myth of Small State Britain — Harry Richer
3

The Welfare Default

The Government Actuary’s Department projects the National Insurance Fund will hit zero by 2043–44 — effectively a default on the welfare state, including the state pension. The triple lock alone is now estimated to cost £15.5 billion a year by 2029, roughly three times the original projections. The system is not underfunded. It was designed for an economy and a demography that no longer exist.

The Catastrophic Consequences of our Government Spending Laid Bare — Liam Noble Shearer
4

The Proof It Can Be Done

Argentina under Milei achieved its first budget surplus in 123 years. Monthly inflation fell from over 140% to under 3%. He cut the state by 30%, eliminated 200 government offices, and removed or lowered 12 federal taxes — while increasing welfare payments to cover 100% of the basic food basket. His approval sits around 50%. The objection that radical spending cuts are politically impossible is contradicted by the one country that has actually tried them.

We Should All Pay Attention to What is Happening in Argentina — Harry Richer
