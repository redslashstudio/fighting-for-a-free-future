THE 60-SECOND POSITION

The British right is split, the Conservative brand is toxic, and Labour governs by default. The question is not whether the right needs to change — it is whether it can find a coherent position before the next election makes the split permanent.

Draft — awaiting editorial review

Why does this matter?

After fourteen years of Conservative government, debt exceeds 100% of GDP, taxes are at a seventy-year high, NHS waiting lists top 7.4 million, and growth has stalled. The public cannot tell the difference between fourteen years of Tory rule and Labour. Meanwhile, Reform UK has risen from a protest vote to a polling force — but with only five MPs, it is arithmetically irrelevant in the current Parliament. The danger is not that the right has no ideas. It is that two parties competing for the same voters hand power to a Labour government that neither set of voters wanted.

What’s actually happening?

The old left-right economic divide is being replaced by a new axis: nationalist-communitarian versus cosmopolitan-globalist. The Conservatives won in 2019 by embracing this realignment — Brexit, Boris, levelling up — but could not deliver on the promise, and those voters defected to Reform. Reform now couples rhetoric about lower taxes with economically interventionist proposals: nationalising utilities, online sales taxes, centrally directed regional investment. From a Hayekian standpoint, this is replacing one form of technocratic planning with another. Baker’s argument is direct: he did not oppose technocracy from Brussels to replace it with technocracy from London. The leading figures in Reform are the same people who presided over the failures they now attack — record immigration, the Online Safety Act, the Brexit deal that bound Britain to the ECHR.

What should change?

The synthesis Baker calls “national liberalism”: free markets and limited government, but within a framework that honours democratic sovereignty and national identity. Sound money as a moral imperative. Wholesale civil service reform. Immigration at levels that sustain social bonds. Economic policy built on production, free trade, deregulation, and cutting energy costs. The communitarian demand for identity and belonging is legitimate — but classical liberalism’s insights about sound money, limited government and the power of free cooperation are what resolve the fiscal crisis. The populist temptation to use state power aggressively leads to authoritarianism and stagnation. The path forward runs through the Conservative Party — renewed, principled, and anchored in the liberal inheritance that made British conservatism distinctive.
